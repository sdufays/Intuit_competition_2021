Identifying and reflecting on unconscious bias


Up until three years ago, whenever CEO, CFO, scientist, or doctor was typed into the textboxes of our iphones, an emoji of a man in a suit was displayed. Shocking right? Even seemingly innocuous concepts like emojis can reinforce gender stereotypes and demonstrate the structural discrimination apparent in our society. However, most people would not even notice these subtle standards that have a much larger impact on society than one might think.


The hidden biases and stereotypes embedded into aspects of our everyday lives perpetuate a vicious cycle that propagates hidden biases we might not even acknowledge. Their name? Implicit and unconscious biases.


But What is Unconscious Bias?


Unconscious biases, also known as implicit biases, are the underlying attitudes and stereotypes that affect our understanding, actions, and decisions in an unconscious manner. Residing deep in the subconscious, they are often unconsciously attributed to another person, or group of people, and affect how we understand and engage with that person or group.


These biases, which encompass both favorable and unfavorable assessments, are activated involuntarily and without an individual’s awareness or intentional control. They are different from known biases that individuals may choose to conceal for the purposes of social and/or political correctness. Everyone possesses them, even people with avowed commitments to impartiality, like judges. Implicit biases are heavily influenced by our background, cultural environment and our personal experiences. The simple concept of implicit bias, introduced in 1995, refutes the common and long standing belief that humans are guided solely by explicits beliefs and our conscious intentions.


Unfortunately, unconscious bias is everywhere. From the people that are closest to us, the doctors that we choose to treat us, the neighborhoods we choose, and the people we date, unconscious categorizing form the basis of our relationship with others, and the world at large.


How common are these biases?


Implicit biases happen automatically and are triggered by our brain making quick judgments and assessments of people and situations. What people often fail to acknowledge is that they are far more prevalent than conscious bias, and often even incompatible with our conscious values.


Don’t believe it? Did you know that a google image search will automatically conclude that 90% of professors are white men? While it is true that there is a large gender disparity with only 25% of women being on a professorial level, the 90% statistic is not only inaccurate, but demonstrates the inherent bias that is embedded into people’s mentalities. Additionally, a study learning from over 100,000 images on the internet has demonstrated that AI algorithms learned to associate women with images of a kitchen.


A Yale University study found that both male and female scientists, believing themselves to be objective, were more likely to hire men due to the implicit belief that they were more competent than women. The University of Chicago participated in a study where thousands of identical resumes were sent out to employers with job openings. They used stereotypically African-American names (such as “Jamal”) on some and stereotypically white names (like “Brendan”) on others. Roughly 50 percent of the same resumes were more likely to result in a call back for an interview if it had a “white” name. Because the résumés were statistically identical, any differences in outcomes could be attributed only to the factor that was manipulated: the name.


When these studies were finalized and shown to the research participants, they were always shocked, as they would often not believe their own internalized biases.


Since 1998, more than 20 million people have taken the Implicit Association Test (IAT), an online assessment at Harvard’s Project Implicit website. Despite the self-proclaimed title embraced by most individuals of being all-inclusive, the data strongly suggest that many people hold implicit biases toward members of particular groups. For example, about 75 percent of whites and Asians demonstrated an implicit bias in favor of whites compared to African Americans.

How do I address my own unconscious biases?


While it is true that we can never entirely rid ourselves of bias, there are certain mentalities we can foster and encourage others to develop that reduce its harmful effects and hindrance of diversity.


The first step in addressing unconscious bias is to deeply exam your personal beliefs, values, attitudes, and perceptions. What experiences have shaped your worldview and personal narrative? How have these experiences affected your interactions with others? Which biases are prevalent in your community? Do you find a small part of yourself agreeing with these perspectives?


According to psychologists, implicit biases are shaped by our lived experiences and learned from images and ideological perspectives that reside in the community we live in. These instances are manifested in our verbal/nonverbal communication, body language, and everyday interactions.


The second step in addressing implicit bias is to refrain from stereotyping others and work on individuation, meaning evaluating people based on their personal characteristics rather than those associated with their group. You can do this by connecting over shared interests and making sure to affiliate with diverse people.


The third step in combating unconscious bias is to inform yourself and spread awareness. Unconscious bias is not necessarily a concept that many people stop to reflect upon or even acknowledge in life. Just the act of being aware of it and its unfavorable effects creates an incentive to push back against this predisposition and against our comfort zone to overcome it.


So research. Talk about it. Spread your knowledge and understanding of implicit bias to reduce the inequity, microaggressions, and stereotyping caused by it.

Did you know that although being thirteen percent of the population, black people are seven more likely than white people to be wrongfully incriminated for murder? This is unfortunately due to attribution bias, often the cause of many inequalities, microaggressions, and microinvalidations.

Affinity Bias


As human beings, we often tend to gravitate towards people similar to ourselves due to a phenomenon denoted as “affinity bias”. This mental shortcut causes us to hold a more favorable opinion of someone who shares similar characteristics to us, whether it be the way we look, our backgrounds, our gender, our shared experiences, or our common interests.

Conformity Bias


Conformity bias is when an individual feels the need to change themselves, whether it be their vocabulary, looks, or beliefs, in order to be better accepted into society or into a group. Conformity is present in many, if not all social settings, especially the workplace and in friendships. While it isn’t fundamentally bad, it has the potential to fabricate unrealistic standards, as well as perpetuate the decline in diversity.

Halo-Horns Effect


The Halo/Horns Effect is an unconscious bias that involves all other biases. It’s when a person’s impression of someone is overly influenced (either positively or negatively) by a single personality quality, physical trait, or experience, which is often arbitrary.
