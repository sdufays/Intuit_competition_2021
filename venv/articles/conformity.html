What is Conformity?

Conformity is the act of changing yourself via one’s physical appearance, language, or mentality to better fit into a social circle. This can manifest in a form of bias, where the individual feels pressure to change themselves or their mannerism to be better received in a social setting, such as African Americans changing their hair to be perceived as more professional.

History of Conformity

Conformity bias was first discovered in the 1900s, however, it has affected humans since the beginning of cooperation between humans. Researchers experimented, asking individuals to place estimates on the number of beans in a bottle. Afterward, they left the individuals to discuss their findings and their estimations. Later, the individuals were asked if they would like to change their estimations, and every participant changed their guesses to be closer to the number the group had discussed. It is built into the human mind to default to the group’s findings for multiple reasons. One, conforming to the group in the past led to a higher chance of survival. If everyone had eaten this plant and not died, why question their judgment and try another plant? Two, following the group, decreases the social pressure as well as judgment you may experience if you end up being wrong in your choice. No one can judge another for getting an action wrong if they all made the same mistake.

Conformity Studies

A famous example of conformity is the Ash Experiment, where teens were given a set of 3 lines, one line being clearly longer than the others. They were then asked to decide which line is the longest. Teens were then lined up with actors that were paid to say a clearly incorrect answer. Every actor said the exact same incorrect answer, and when it came to the teen subject’s turn to recite their answer, in fear of choosing the wrong answer and wandering from the expectation, they switched from the correct answer to the incorrect one chosen by the actors. This isn’t a coincidence, as every teen in the experiment exhibited the exact same behavior. When asked afterward, every teen described their simple conclusions to the correct answer, but how they felt compelled to choose the wrong answer in fear of alienation and judgment from the group and from breaking the precedent the group had set.


How Conformity Can Be Harmful

Conformity isn’t inherently bad, as shown through the evolution of human practices as well as in present-day scenarios. People are bound to change once exposed to different personalities and experiences throughout life. The beliefs you start with will not be the same beliefs you always hold; that’s just a part of gaining knowledge and growing as a human. However, it’s when someone feels the need to change themselves to feel accepted into a group that conformity shifts to its negative connotation. This is seen with physical changes, such as hair texture and length, along with wardrobes, such as hijabs or turbans, and finally mental changes, such as altering your accent, your slang, or your opinion; all of which aim to make yourself more desirable and wanted in a social setting. It’s here where psychological trauma and self-loathing are born, with individuals internalizing the idea that they must change to be good enough and that right now they are flawed and must change. This is a breeding ground for mental issues, such as eating disorders and depression, which are feelings that no one should face.


Ways to Help:

  • Encourage diversity: Conformity stems from the desire to fit in, so creating a workspace or social situation that rewards diverse thoughts, beliefs, and appearances can encourage those insecure about themselves to find the courage to express themselves, without fear of judgment. .


  • Encourage skepticism: The question, “why?” is a powerful one and it allows people to examine the reasons they do an action quite effectively. By questioning the choices you make, to ensure that you do them with the best intentions at heart, we can ensure that no one ever feels pressured to act a certain way to acquiesce to a standard they believe they must follow.


  • Group size: Conformity in ideas can occur under the pressure of a large group. Breaking groups up into those of 2-4 can be helpful in combating the pressure a larger crowd can elicit.


  • Encourage “risks” and be clear––this isn’t to say that people should actively seek out actions that they know to be wrong or incorrect. However, many people view voicing ideas and sentiments, that they aren’t completely sure of, as a “risk.” Encouraging these “risks” can do two things. First, it creates a space where it is deemed okay to mess up and make the wrong call. This allows people to be more relaxed in taking a chance and voicing their beliefs. Second, making mistakes decreases the ambiguity of situations, as it teaches people what the correct, unbiased expectations are. Ambiguity is linked to an increase in conformity, as those who have an unclear expectation about what the “correct answer” may be, are less likely to participate in the discussion for fear of voicing an “incorrect answer.” Clearing up ambiguity is key to decreasing conformity as it creates a more comfortable environment to test ideas while encouraging free thought.



Conformity is a serious issue that plagues many around the world. With these tips, even if you don’t actively practice conformity, you can make the world a comfortable, more colorful place.
